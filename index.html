America is aging. Are more Diversity Visas the Answer?

In an aging society, young immigrants are needed more than ever.

By Khushali Haji

The number of Americans ages 65 and older will more than double over the next 40 years, reaching 80 million in 2040 (21.6% of the population). This change in demographics could be combated by increasing immigration.

The diversity immigrant visa program (DV program, sometimes called “the green card lottery” or “the visa lottery”) came into effect in 1995 to stimulate “new seed” immigration, or to foster new, more varied migration from other parts of the world. The distribution of diversity visas by global region of origin has shifted over time, with higher shares coming from Africa and Asia in recent years compared to earlier years when Europe accounted for a higher proportion.



“There is no doubt that the U.S. needs more workers. We are an aging society.” said Muzzafar Chisti, director at the Migration Policy Institute, who was also involved in the legislation that led to diversity visas. “We need young, active workers to beef up our social security system that is under strain, to fill gaps in the labor market.”



In many ways the demographics of America is changing. Currently, the program currently makes 50,000 visas available annually to natives of countries which do not have representation in the United States. Chisti feels this isn’t enough.

“We must increase legal immigration by twice if not three times,” he said. “Only immigration will help us contend those challenges.”
